\subsection{$\beta$ functions of fractional $\phi^4$ in $d=4$}
In order to explore the space of theories around the fractional Gaussian fixed point, we introduce interactions to the C-type theory. The preceding analysis indicates that any deformation of this fixed point will be non-local in the sense that it will be impossible to find a localizing field redefinition. 

\begin{equation}
    S = \int _{\mathbb{R}^d} \phi(-\Delta)^\gamma  \phi - \sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}_{even}^+}\frac{g_n}{n!}\phi^n.
\end{equation}

  It is an elementary exercise in the background field expansion method to show that the $\beta$ functions to one loop order are given by

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \beta&(g_n) = g_n (d-n(2\gamma-d)/2)-\\
    &\text{Vol}(S^{d-1})\Lambda^{n(d-2\gamma)/2}\frac{\delta^n \log(\Lambda^{2\gamma}+V_\gamma''[\varphi])}{\delta \varphi^n}\bigg\vert_{\varphi=0}+...
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
For simplicity we specialize to the case of $d=4$ where only terms up to the $\phi^4$ interaction are relevant. The RG equations to one loop are
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \beta_\gamma (g_2) &= -2 \gamma  g_2-\frac{g_4 \text{Vol}(S^3)}{g_2+1}+...\\
        \beta_\gamma (g_4) &=(d-4\gamma)g_4+\frac{3\text{Vol}(S^3)g_4^2}{(g_2+1)^2}+...
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
One should note immediately that $(\beta_\gamma(g_2),\beta_\gamma(g_2))=0$ has a non-trivial solution:
\begin{equation}
    g_2^*= -\frac{2 (\gamma -1)}{5 \gamma -2}+...,\hspace{2mm}g_4^*= \frac{12 \left(\gamma ^3-\gamma ^2\right)}{(5 \gamma -2)^2 \text{Vol}(S^3)}+...
\end{equation}

In the limit where $\gamma \to 1 $, this fixed point merges with the Gaussian fixed point as expected in dimension 4. It  is clear that the introduction of $0<\gamma <1$ modifies the theory in the same way as the  $\epsilon$ expansion does. Thus, this new fixed point is exactly analogous to the Wilson-Fischer fixed point. \COMMENT{cite ppl} The string of massless theories that connect the two fixed points of the theory have interesting implications in the context of non-locality: While the Gaussian fixed point is local, there is a subspace of non-local interacting critical theories connecting the two fixed points. With the standard signs chosen above, the non-trivial fixed point is not physically acceptable as it leads to an unstable theory. The replacement $g_4\to -g_4$ exhibits the standard perturbative RG structure of the $\phi^4$ theory in $d-\epsilon$ but with explicit implications on the locality of theories. 

In this  prototypical example the UV physics is governed by an apparently local fractional theory while the IR fixed point that would usually be ascribed to an Ising CFT is now a non-local, non-trivial IR theory. \COMMENT{shouldn't our theory be local in the UV then? \subsection{Replica trick and geometric entropy}

The form of the EE in Eq.~(\ref{eq:VN}) is not amenable to analytical computation. Instead we consider
\begin{equation}
    S_N=-(\partial_N -1)\log \text{Tr}\rho_A^N.
\end{equation}
This quantity is more tractable and lends itself well to a geometric interpretation.  Formally, one requires the existence of a unique analytic continuation of this function to non-integer $N$ before letting $N\to 1$ and hence recovering the (von Neumann) EE. If such a unique continuation exists, the computation of the EE amounts to determining $\text{Tr}\rho_A^N$ which we identify as the partition function defined on the $N$-cover with a branch along $A$ as well as conical singularities on $\partial A$. For a Gaussian theory on $\mathbb{R}^d$, this space is the flat cone, $C_\delta$ with deficit angle $\delta=2\pi(1-N)$. The choice of a non-local differential operator in the action poses no difficulty in terms of diagonalization in replica space.   For simplicity and because the entropy should not depend on the underlying geometry, we take a hyperplane rather than a general hyper-surface as the entangling surface.  The quantity of interest is then\cite{wilczek,heatkernel,callanGeometricEntropy1994}
\beq
    S_\delta=-(2\pi \partial_\delta +1)\log Z_\delta,
\eeq
and the limit to obtain the EE is $\delta \to 0$.
